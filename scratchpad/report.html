Other gaps between the warehouse & shipping sides

Already connected: orders are one shared list · product catalog is shared · the box catalog is now one list · shipping a label auto-decrements WMS stock and fires low-stock alerts · pick/packing-slip documents share templates.

Remaining, ranked

  1. Pick-list workflow objects aren’t linked — the biggest one. Shipping-side pick lists print; WMS pick lists are tracked. They share the paper, not the process.
  2. Returns don’t restock automatically — a return on the shipping side doesn’t yet add the unit back to WMS on-hand.
  3. Ship-from addresses vs. WMS warehouses are two lists — same physical place, maintained twice.
  4. No single inventory ledger showing every reason a number moved (sold, shipped, received, counted, returned) in one place.

None are on fire. #1 and #2 are the two I’d do next — deliberately, not overnight.

Pick lists & packing slips — tying the two sides together

Shipping side

Stateless print helpers — aggregate the current batch and print. Nothing tracked afterward.

Warehouse side

A tracked workflow object — created, assigned, scanned item-by-item, then pack-verified. Has status and history.

To connect them: when WMS mode is on, make the shipping-side “Pick list” button create a WMS pick-list object instead of just printing — so a batch queued to ship becomes a real, trackable pick task, and pack-verify closes the loop back to the label. A focused project, not an overnight change — it rewires a live workflow.

Dropoffs & Mail Boxes — do you need them?

  • Dropoffs — an intake log for packages a walk-in customer hands you to ship (pending → shipped).
  • Mail Boxes — a rental registry: who rents which physical mailbox, and its status.

Both are retail counter tools. If you’re 3PL / ecommerce, they’re clutter — keep them behind a “front-desk” toggle that’s off by default. If a client runs a retail shipping storefront, they’re exactly right. Each now has an in-app Help section that says the same, so anyone who opens them understands what they’re for.
